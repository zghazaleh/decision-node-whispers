# 00 — Product Philosophy

## Why Decision Nodes exists

Most software that touches "decisions" optimizes the *answer*. Spreadsheets compute outcomes. Coaching apps push frameworks. Games hand out wins and losses. Chatbots flatter the user into agreement.

Decision Nodes exists because the interesting part of a decision is not the answer. It is the **reasoning under pressure** — what you noticed, what you ignored, what you assumed, when you updated, and how you held your nerve. That layer is almost never inspected, and almost never preserved.

Decision Nodes preserves it.

## The problem it is solving

Consequential decisions in real life share four properties:

1. The full information is never available.
2. Time and social pressure distort judgment.
3. Outcomes are noisy — good reasoning sometimes loses, poor reasoning sometimes wins.
4. The person making the decision rarely gets an honest, structured read of *how* they thought.

Books, courses, and post-mortems try to address this, but they are abstract. The lessons rarely transfer because the reader was never inside the dilemma. Decision Nodes puts the player inside a dilemma, lets them feel the pressure, and then — only after they commit — turns the lens back on the reasoning itself.

## What it is fundamentally not

- **Not a game.** Games have score, win-state, leaderboards, replayable optimal paths. Decision Nodes has none of these.
- **Not a chatbot.** Chatbots serve the user. The Director serves the *story* — it withholds, refuses, stays in character.
- **Not a simulation.** Simulations model systems. Decision Nodes models *situations* — finite, authored, with hidden truths.
- **Not educational software.** Educational software teaches a curriculum. Decision Nodes never tells you the answer; it reflects your reasoning back to you and lets you draw your own conclusions.

The closest analog is literary: a short story you can talk to, that watches you decide, and then writes the only honest review of you that exists.

## The long-term vision

A growing archive of consequential decisions — authored, then community-authored — that anyone can step into. Over time, each player accumulates a **Decision Profile**: not a score, but a portrait of how they think. The archive becomes a public library of dilemmas; the profiles become a private mirror.

The product is finished when a thoughtful person, anywhere in the world, can spend an hour inside a difficult situation and come out understanding their own mind a little better. Everything else — UI, models, prompts, infrastructure — is in service of that hour.
