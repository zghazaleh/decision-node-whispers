# 06 — World Building

A Decision Nodes must feel like a real situation, not a puzzle. This document describes how the product creates that feeling and what every case author must respect.

## Immersion

Immersion is built in the first thirty seconds and lost easily after. The opening must:

- Place the player as a specific person, not a viewpoint.
- Put another named human in front of them, mid-sentence.
- Establish that something has already happened, and that something else is about to.

The scene image, the audio bed, and the opening text all arrive together. None of them are decorative; each carries part of the establishment.

## Identity

The player is given a role, not a blank slate. They are Dr. Vasquez. They are the CFO. They are the one who has to walk into that room. The product never asks the player to "be themselves" — it asks them to inhabit someone whose decision matters.

## NPC realism

Characters are real to the extent that they:

- Want things that do not perfectly align with the player's interests.
- Know things they will not volunteer.
- Speak in their own register — Sarah does not sound like Marcus does not sound like Amara.
- Refuse, evade, deflect, or change the subject when a real person would.

A character who answers every question fully and directly is a quest-giver, not a person. Quest-givers do not belong in Decision Nodes.

## Time pressure

Pressure is *felt*, not enforced. There is no countdown on the screen. Pressure rises through:

- The accumulating ambient cue, which intensifies with conversation length.
- The character writing, which gets tighter and more weighted as turns go on.
- The structural awareness that a Decide control is sitting on screen the entire time, waiting.

A visible timer would replace psychological tension with mechanical anxiety. That is a different product.

## Information discovery

Every meaningful fact must be reachable through a natural in-world action: a question to the right person, an examination of the right object, a follow-up on a half-finished sentence. Nothing is unlocked through menus. Nothing is highlighted as "the important clue." The player must do the noticing.

## Hidden knowledge

Every Decision Nodes must contain knowledge that is *available but not delivered*. If a player can finish a case without ever finding a particular truth, that truth was correctly hidden. The Analyzer's `evidenceIgnored` field exists precisely so the player can later see what was reachable.

## Tension

Tension comes from competing legitimate interests, not from villains. Every case should be authored so that a thoughtful person can defend every preset archetype. If one stance is obviously correct, the case is not a Decision Nodes — it is a quiz.

## Player agency

The player owns their decision absolutely. The product never:

- Prevents the player from making a "wrong" choice.
- Re-routes the player toward an authored ideal.
- Replays or unwinds a commit.

Agency without consequence is not agency. The authored consequence timeline is the product's promise that the player's choice mattered.

## What breaks a world

- A character speaking out of voice.
- A canon fact contradicted in dialogue.
- A UI element that names the game ("you have 2 turns left").
- Coaching prose appearing inside the scene.
- A "tutorial" overlay explaining how to play.

Each of these has happened in other products. Each is forbidden here.
