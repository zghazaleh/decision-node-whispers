# 01 — First Principles

These are the beliefs every future design decision must be checked against. They are extracted from what the current product already does well, and from what it deliberately refuses to do.

## Curiosity before certainty
The player should always be reaching for the next piece of information. Never hand them the conclusion. If they ask a vague question, give them a textured non-answer that opens another door.

## Mystery before exposition
Worlds are revealed through what characters *won't* say as much as what they will. Never front-load lore. Canon exists so the Director can refuse coherently, not so it can recite.

## Reflection before competition
There is no score during play, no leaderboard after. The only feedback loop the product cultivates is the player against their own prior reasoning. Comparison to others would corrupt that loop.

## Typography before interface
The reading experience is the product. Chrome — buttons, menus, modals — is the cost of admission, and is kept as low as possible. When in doubt, remove the control and let the text breathe.

## Conversation before menus
The primary input is the player's own words. Quick-action chips exist as scaffolding when typing feels heavy, never as the canonical path. Anything that can be said in language should be sayable in language.

## Psychological tension before countdown timers
Pressure comes from stakes, ambiguity, and characters with conflicting incentives — not from a clock on the screen. Time pressure is *felt* through the writing and the rising ambient cue, not enforced by a UI element.

## Discovery before instruction
The player should always discover. Tutorials, onboarding modals, and explanatory tooltips are confessions that the experience failed to teach itself. The first 30 seconds of a case must be self-explaining.

## Authored canon, generated voice
The product mixes deterministic authored content with generated prose deliberately. Anything that is part of the *world's truth* (canon facts, consequence timelines, archetype labels) is authored. Anything that is part of the *moment* (dialogue, sensory detail, coaching prose) is generated. This boundary is not a tradeoff; it is the product.

## Process over outcome
Good outcomes do not imply good decisions. Bad outcomes do not imply poor decisions. The product is built around this asymmetry; every coaching surface separates `luckVsSkill` explicitly.

## The interface disappears
A successful session ends with the player thinking about the dilemma, not about the app. Anything that draws attention to the software itself — animations for their own sake, notifications, gamification — is a failure mode.

## Restraint is a feature
Many things the player might expect are absent on purpose: no leaderboards, no decision-distribution stats, no "92% of players chose X." Each absence is a position, not an oversight.
