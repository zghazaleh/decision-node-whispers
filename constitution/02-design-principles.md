# 02 — Design Principles

The visual language of Decision Nodes is editorial, not gamified. The reference point is a literary magazine or a long-form essay, not a dashboard or a game HUD.

## Editorial aesthetic

The page should feel composed, not assembled. Headings, body, and supporting metadata sit in a clear hierarchy and respect classical print proportions. Components borrow from book and magazine design: generous margins, restrained rules, deliberate caps, italicized voice.

## Quiet interface

Every control earns its pixels. Buttons are present but unobtrusive; controls fade until needed. The chrome around the conversation is a frame, not the picture. If a UI element is competing with the writing, the writing wins.

## Typography is the system

Type carries the brand, the mood, and most of the meaning. A display serif for character names and titles. A clean sans for body. Italics for sensory beats — never bold for emphasis in narrative passages. Letter-spacing on labels is tightened to feel printed. The line length on prose is bounded so the reading experience does not collapse on wide screens.

## Motion philosophy

Motion is used to *settle* the eye, not to entertain it. Fades and gentle reveals where new content arrives. No bouncing, no springs that overshoot, no decorative loops. Sound and motion together carry rising tension — neither alone.

## Color philosophy

A muted, low-saturation palette derived from the scene image of the case. Backgrounds tend toward deep neutral, foregrounds toward warm paper-white. Color is used to mark *meaning* (a decision committed, an archetype identified, a closing tone) rather than to decorate. There is no brand neon; there is no purple gradient.

## White space

White space is the most powerful design element in the product. It is what makes the writing feel considered. Reducing white space to fit more on screen is almost always the wrong tradeoff; the right tradeoff is to show less.

## Information density

Low density on the surface, high density in the writing. The screen at any moment should feel scannable in three seconds; the *content* on the screen should reward three minutes of attention. Dashboards, stats panels, and multi-column layouts violate this and are avoided.

## Cinematic pacing

A case is paced like a short film, not a level. The opening is deliberately slow; the middle accumulates pressure; the decision modal arrives as an interruption; the analysis screen is a coda. Audio, scene image, and writing all tighten together — none in isolation. The player should feel the act breaks even if they cannot name them.

## Why these choices exist

The product is asking the player to take an hour of their attention and turn it inward. Anything that feels like an app — bright color, gamified feedback, busy layout — pulls them back out. The visual restraint is not minimalism for fashion; it is the only register in which the rest of the product can do its work.
